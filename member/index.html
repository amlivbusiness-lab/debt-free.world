❄️ Snowball vs 🔥 Avalanche

Two proven debt strategies — here's which fits your situation.

❄️ Snowball method
Pay the smallest debt first, regardless of interest rate. Builds momentum with quick wins. Best if you need motivation to stay committed.
🔥 Avalanche method
Pay the highest interest debt first. Saves the most money long-term. Best if you have high-interest credit cards or consumer loans.
🗺️ Debt Collection Process Map

Understanding the process removes the fear. Know what comes next — and your rights at each stage.

  1. 1Missed paymentYou miss one payment. The creditor sends a reminder. No negative credit impact yet.
  2. 2Overdue notice14–30 days: formal overdue letter with fees. Respond in writing — this is the best time to negotiate.
  3. 3Debt passed to collections60–90 days: creditor sells or transfers debt to a collection agency. Your rights remain the same.
  4. 4Collection lettersAgency contacts you by letter and phone. You can request all communication in writing only.
  5. 5Legal action threatAgency may threaten court proceedings. Often a negotiation tactic — many settle before court.
  6. 6Court judgmentIf no agreement: court may issue a payment order. You have the right to dispute it within the deadline.
  7. 7EnforcementOnly after a court judgment. Enforcement agency may garnish wages or bank accounts. Negotiate a payment plan immediately.

🔑 Your rights at every stage

  • Request all communication in writing
  • Request proof that the debt is valid and belongs to you
  • Negotiate a payment plan — creditors usually prefer this over legal action
  • Use our AI Letter generator to respond professionally
🎯 90-Day Debt-Free Challenge

Small consistent actions compound. Here's your framework.

Week 1–2: Clarity

  • List every debt: creditor, balance, rate, minimum payment
  • Calculate your exact monthly surplus (income − all costs)
  • Decide: Snowball or Avalanche?
  • Write one letter to your highest-priority creditor using the AI tool

Week 3–4: First wins

  • Make your first extra payment, however small
  • Cancel one non-essential subscription
  • Set up a separate "debt" savings pot — even 10 €/month matters
  • Track: screenshot your balance

Month 2: Build momentum

  • Review and renegotiate one debt's interest rate
  • Find one additional income source (sell something, extra hours)
  • Apply the Avalanche or Snowball payment to next priority debt
  • Update your debt-free date estimate

Month 3: Systemise

  • Automate your extra debt payment
  • Set a 6-month milestone review date
  • Celebrate progress — you've changed your financial trajectory
🧠 Behavioural & Habit Tools

Impulse spending control

  • 24-hour rule: any non-essential purchase over 20 € waits 24 hours
  • Cost-per-hour: convert prices to hours worked — "this costs 3 hours of my life"
  • Remove friction: delete saved card details from online shops
  • Replace the trigger: identify what emotion precedes spending — find a free alternative action

Financial habit building

  • Schedule a weekly 15-minute money check — same time, same day
  • Keep a spending diary for 2 weeks — awareness alone reduces spending 10–15%
  • Use the "pay yourself first" rule: move debt payment on payday, not end of month

Financial stress management

  • Separate "debt admin time" from your personal life — check once a week, not constantly
  • Write down your worst-case scenario fully — most fears are less catastrophic when explicit
  • Identify one person you can talk to openly — social support is the strongest protective factor
  • If stress is affecting your health: contact your GP. Financial stress is a medical concern.
🆘 Crisis Action Plans

If you've just lost your job

  1. Register as unemployed immediately — benefits often start from day 1 of registration, not job loss
  2. Contact your creditors within the first 2 weeks — before you miss a payment
  3. Request a payment pause or reduced minimum — most lenders have hardship processes
  4. List all income sources: unemployment benefit, partner income, side income
  5. Use the AI Letter tool: "Hardship / payment difficulty"

If you're going through divorce/separation

  1. Get a written list of all joint debts and accounts
  2. Freeze joint credit card limits if possible
  3. Contact each creditor to separate or reassign joint debts
  4. Prioritise housing cost — roof first, everything else second
  5. Seek free legal advice on debt liability split before signing anything

If you're on sick leave / unable to work

  1. Check your sick pay entitlement — statutory and employer
  2. Check insurance policies: some cover loan payments during illness
  3. Contact creditors immediately with a doctor's letter — most have compassion policies
  4. Apply for disability benefits if absence will exceed 3 months
  5. Use "Hardship" letter from the AI tool

Emergency kit — do this now if in crisis

  • Pay rent/mortgage first — everything else is negotiable, housing is not
  • Keep food and utilities on — call utility providers for payment plans
  • Stop all non-essential direct debits today
  • Contact National Debt Helpline or local equivalent (free, confidential)
  • Do not ignore letters — open everything, date-stamp, keep copies
🏦 Bank Guides & Monthly Updates

Negotiating with your bank

  • Always ask to speak with the hardship or arrears team — not general customer service
  • Prepare before calling: debt amount, monthly income, monthly expenses, what you can realistically pay
  • Ask specifically for: reduced interest rate, payment holiday, extended repayment term, or balance settlement
  • Get any agreement in writing before ending the call
  • Use our AI Letter tool to follow up in writing after every call

What banks can offer (most will, if asked)

  • Payment holiday: 1–3 months with no payment required
  • Reduced minimum payment: pay interest only for a period
  • Interest freeze: for customers in genuine hardship
  • Reduced settlement: offer 40–70% of balance for full settlement
